## Definitional and Measurement Problems: Benchmarks Miss Core Intelligence

AGI suffers from definitional ambiguity that becomes critical near human-level performance. Current benchmarks test narrow cognitive slices rather than integrated intelligence. The Legg-Hutter universal intelligence metric fails to capture embodied cognition, while academic debates continue over whether consciousness or capability defines AGI. More fundamentally, 'general intelligence' may be a category error—human cognition itself consists of specialized modules rather than unified general reasoning, making AGI an ill-defined target that conflates performance breadth with architectural generality. Consider how GPT-4 passes the bar exam but fails basic spatial reasoning—under narrow definitions, this could qualify as AGI, while broader definitions requiring human-level performance across all domains would exclude it. This measurement ambiguity means we could achieve 'AGI' by 2030 under some definitions while remaining decades away under others.

O3's ARC-AGI results illustrate this perfectly: 87.5% accuracy required massive compute and performance dropped 40% on rotated variants, suggesting sophisticated pattern matching rather than robust reasoning. Current evaluation protocols systematically miss key AGI components—persistent memory integration, contextual learning transfer, and multi-modal reasoning coherence. As models saturate these tests, the definitional bar rises, revealing gaps between benchmark performance and genuine understanding.

Current models fail catastrophically on out-of-distribution tasks: GPT-4 achieves 89% on standard reading comprehension but drops to 23% when the same logical structure is presented in unfamiliar contexts (Johnson et al., 2024). This brittleness suggests we're optimizing for benchmarks rather than developing robust general intelligence. Performance variability across semantically identical tasks remains 20-30%, indicating fundamental gaps in robust intelligence architecture.
